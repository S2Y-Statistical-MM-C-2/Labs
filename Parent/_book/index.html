#The Basic Premise of Hypothsis Testing

All hypothesis tests that we will look at in these labs will fit the same pattern:

We want to decide if the observed data from our study provides evidence to reject a baseline assumption (a null hypothesis).

For all tests, we will compare our observed data to an expected distribution from the null hypothesis.

The test will have a significance level and we find the critical value that corresponds to this to decide on a Rejection Region (shown in red on the graph below). This is the part of our distribution that we decide is unusual and could disprove our null hypothesis.

We calculate a Standardised Test Statistic from our data and find its corresponding area on the distribution, the p-value, to be able to compare what we saw to the distribution (illustrated in blue on the graph below).

How the Standardised Test Statistic compares to the rejection region, and hence how our p-value compares to the significance level, is what changes the conclusion of our hypothesis test.

The graphical example above is a test that would assume normally distributed data and would result in failing to reject the null hypothesis. The red area is our rejection region. The blue area represents the probability of seeing our observed data assuming the null hypothesis is true (this probability is known as the p-value). The Standardised Test Statistic does not sit in the rejection region. As a result the blue area is greater than the red area meaning our data was not unusual compared to the distribution where the null hypothesis is true.

#The 5 steps of Hypothesis Testing

In the next few labs we will look through different scenarios where we would complete hypothesis tests.

In your textbook and lectures you have seen these tests broken into 5 simple steps

  1. Hypotheses
  • Set up the hypotheses to answer your question
  1. Test Statistic
  • Chose and calculate your Test Statistic using your observed data
  • Identify and calculate your Standardised Test Statistic using your pbserved data
  1. Rejection Region Calculations
  • Find the Critical Value from the distribution
  1. Statistical Conclusion
  • Find your p-value that corresponds to your Standardised Test Statistic
  • Compare your Standardised Test Statistic to the Rejection Region or Critical value
  • Compare your p-value to the significance level
  1. English Conclusion
  • Give the meaning of statistical conclusion in context of the question.

Often when undertaking hypothesis tests in R, we find the Standardised Test Statistic and p-value in one action such as when using t.test() .

For this reason the 5 steps are broken up slightly differently in these labs so that we set up our test in entirety by hand and find the values from the distribution first. This gives us everything we need to compare our data to, then we run the analysis on our observed, giving us our Standardised Test Statistic and p-value.

  1. Hypotheses
  • Set up the hypotheses to answer your question
  1. Test Statistic
  • Choose your Test Statistic
  1. Hypothesis Test Calculations
  • Find the Critical Value and Rejection Region from the distribution
  • Calculate your Standardised Test Statistic from the observed data
  • Find your p-value that corresponds to your Standardised Test Statistic
  1. Statistical Conclusion
  • Comparing your Standardised Test Statistic to the Rejection Region or Critical value
  • Comparing your p-value to the significance level
  1. English Conclusion
  • Giving the meaning of statistical conclusion in context of the question.
